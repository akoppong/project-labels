# Low-Ops SEO Blogging Plan

Date: 2026-06-20
Product: `csvtolabels.com`
Goal: drive qualified SEO traffic with AI-assisted blogging while keeping code ownership and operational overhead as low as possible.

## 1. Decision summary

- Keep the product app in the existing Next.js codebase.
- Do not build a full blog or CMS into this repo.
- Run the blog inside the existing Next.js app under `/blog`.
- Recommended platform: `Next.js + local MDX on Vercel`.
- Best paid alternative: `Ghost(Pro)` if editorial convenience becomes more important than cost.

## 2. Why this fits the current business

The current repo and business plan already assume a narrow utility with low support burden:

- the app is mostly static and server-rendered
- the product wedge is tightly defined
- the business case weakens if the organization takes on support-heavy systems
- the launch plan does not depend on SEO for the first 21 days

That means the blogging system should help long-term acquisition without changing the operating model of the product. A repo-owned MDX blog keeps the product and content on one domain without adding another paid service or hosting surface.

## 3. Platform ranking

### 1. Next.js + local MDX on `csvtolabels.com/blog`

Best overall fit.

Why:

- no extra paid platform
- strongest same-domain SEO cohesion
- no separate hosting layer beyond Vercel
- local MDX works cleanly with static generation
- metadata, internal links, and sitemap logic can stay in the same app

Tradeoff:

- the organization owns content files in the repo
- editorial convenience is lower than a dedicated CMS UI

Use this if the primary goal is the lowest combined cost and operational overhead.

### 2. Ghost(Pro) on `blog.csvtolabels.com`

Best if editorial convenience matters more than cost.

Why:

- managed hosting
- built-in SEO features
- simple publishing UI
- custom domain support
- API and integrations available without building a custom backend

Tradeoff:

- extra paid account
- blog lives on a subdomain instead of the main product path
- editor and product analytics are split across tools

Use this only if the MDX workflow becomes too inconvenient.

### 3. WordPress.com on `blog.csvtolabels.com`

Best if AI-native CMS operations are the top priority.

Why:

- managed hosting
- automatic sitemap generation
- AI-generated SEO metadata
- recent AI-agent integrations can reduce editorial friction

Tradeoff:

- more CMS surface area
- higher risk of process and settings sprawl over time
- still another service to manage

Use this if you want stronger editing UX than MDX and do not mind another platform.

### 4. Webflow CMS

Strong product, weaker fit for this use case.

Why:

- good managed hosting
- good SEO tooling
- useful AI features

Tradeoff:

- more design-oriented than needed for a utility product
- not the simplest operating model

Use only if the brand and design system become a major differentiator.

## 4. Recommended operating architecture

### Core setup

- `csvtolabels.com`: product app and blog
- `/blog/*`: content routes inside the same Next.js app
- Google Search Console: connected to the root domain
- Google Analytics or lightweight analytics: shared measurement layer across product and blog

### Content ownership

- AI writes first drafts
- human reviews drafts in batches
- repo commits publish the content
- Vercel deploys the content with the app

### Publishing rule

Do not auto-publish directly from AI.

The low-overhead target is not the same as zero review. Full auto-publishing creates quality risk, factual risk, and brand risk that are expensive to unwind. The right model is automated draft generation plus lightweight batch review.

## 5. Practical self-running workflow

### Weekly workflow

1. Pull keyword backlog from one spreadsheet or one repo-owned topic queue.
2. Generate article draft package with AI.
3. Save the draft as a local `.mdx` file.
4. Run deterministic content checks plus repo verification checks.
5. Review once per week for 20 to 30 minutes.
6. Publish 3 to 5 posts in one batch through the normal deploy path.
7. Check Search Console and product analytics once per week.

### Draft package generated by AI

Each article draft should include:

- target keyword
- search intent
- working title
- URL slug
- outline
- article body
- FAQ section
- meta title
- meta description
- 3 internal link suggestions
- 1 product CTA
- 1 image brief for a simple header image

### Operational rule

Keep the workflow in one automation tool only if automation is added at all.

Good choices:

- Zapier
- Make
- n8n if already used elsewhere

Do not build a custom orchestration service for this stage. Manual AI-assisted drafting plus deterministic checks is acceptable and often simpler at this phase.

## 6A. Deterministic checks

The low-overhead system still needs machine-verifiable gates.

Required content checks:

- frontmatter schema is valid
- slug matches filename
- no duplicate slugs
- required metadata fields exist
- article contains at least one product CTA
- article contains at least two internal links
- article does not contain forbidden unsupported claims

Required repo checks:

- blog-content validation script passes
- `npm run lint` passes
- `npm test` passes
- `npm run build` passes

If the repo gains a canonical `npm run verify`, use that as the publish contract.

## 7. AI writing system

### Content constraints

Every article should:

- solve one narrow workflow problem
- match commercial or workflow intent
- link back to the tool naturally
- avoid inflated claims
- clearly state when official UPC or GS1 identifiers are out of scope

### House style

- practical
- short paragraphs
- direct instructions
- examples from CSV, SKU, Shopify export, and Avery 5160 workflows
- no generic SEO filler

### Draft prompt template

Use a prompt structure like this:

```text
Write a search-intent-matched blog post for csvtolabels.com.

Target keyword: {keyword}
Audience: ecommerce sellers and small inventory operators using spreadsheets
Product: a tool that turns CSV rows into printable Avery 5160 Code 128 barcode labels
Intent: {intent}
Primary CTA: preview barcode labels from CSV

Requirements:
- explain the workflow clearly
- include one concrete example
- mention when the user should use GS1 for official UPC needs
- avoid generic history/background filler
- use short sections and direct language
- include a FAQ with 3 questions
- include a meta title under 60 characters
- include a meta description under 155 characters
- suggest 3 internal links to other relevant articles or product pages
```

### Review checklist

Before publishing, check only:

- factual correctness
- clear product CTA
- no unsupported claims
- no hallucinated product features
- no weak filler sections
- slug and metadata match the query

If a draft fails two or more checks, discard it instead of repairing it heavily.

The blog should support the product wedge, not become a generic barcode publication.

### Priority content types

4. FAQ and objection pages

## 10. Internal linking model

Each article should link to:

- the homepage tool
- one related workflow article
- one disclaimer or educational page when relevant

Suggested structure:

- top-third CTA to the product
- in-body contextual link to a related article
- bottom CTA to preview labels now

Do not build large blog navigation systems at this stage. Keep the architecture shallow.

## 11. Success metrics

Track only the metrics that influence decisions:

- impressions by article
- clicks by article
- article visits to product app
- product preview starts from blog traffic
- export clicks from blog traffic
- purchases from blog traffic

Ignore vanity metrics like total published posts or average time on page unless they correlate with conversion.

## 12. Monthly operating cadence

### Week 1

- generate 10 drafts
- review and publish 3 to 5

### Week 2

- refresh titles and metadata on weak pages
- generate 5 more drafts

### Week 3

- publish another 3 to 5
- add internal links from new posts to existing posts

### Week 4

- review Search Console data
- kill weak topics
- expand on posts that are gaining impressions

## 13. Kill criteria

Reassess the blogging system if, after 90 days:

- the content gets impressions but almost no product clicks
- posts require more than 2 hours per week of manual cleanup
- AI drafts are too low quality to review quickly
- blog traffic is broad and informational rather than workflow-intent

If these appear, narrow the content set further instead of expanding into more volume.

## 14. What not to build

Do not build any of the following now:

- custom editorial dashboard
- in-app blog CMS
- custom article database
- custom queueing or orchestration service
- programmatic SEO at scale
- multilingual publishing system
- automated backlink workflow software

Each of these increases overhead before the business has earned it.

## 15. Final recommendation

If the priority is a self-running application with the least operational overhead:

- choose `Next.js + local MDX on Vercel`
- run the blog under `/blog`
- automate draft creation only if it remains simpler than manual drafting
- review and publish in weekly batches
- keep content tightly tied to the CSV-to-label workflow
- run deterministic content and repo checks before publishing

If editorial convenience becomes more important than cost, evaluate `Ghost(Pro)` second.

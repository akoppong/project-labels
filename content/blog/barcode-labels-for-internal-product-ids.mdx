Many businesses assign their own identifiers to products before those products ever interact with retail systems. These internal IDs might be part numbers, stock codes, warehouse references, or any other string that uniquely identifies an item within your own system.

Generating barcode labels for these identifiers is straightforward. You do not need GS1 registration, UPC codes, or any external approval. You encode your own IDs into Code 128 barcodes and print them on standard label sheets.

## Internal IDs versus retail barcodes

This distinction matters when choosing the right approach:

**Internal product IDs** are identifiers you create and control. They are meaningful within your own inventory system. A handheld scanner in your warehouse can read them, your inventory management software can look them up, and your team uses them to find and track products.

**Retail barcodes (UPC, EAN, GTIN)** are standardized identifiers issued through GS1. Retailers require these on product packaging for shelf placement and point-of-sale scanning. They are globally unique across the retail supply chain.

If you are labeling products for internal use only (your own storage, your own pick-and-pack, your own stockroom), you do not need retail barcodes. Your internal IDs are the right choice.

## What barcode format to use

Code 128 is the standard choice for internal product IDs. It supports:

- Alphanumeric strings (letters and numbers mixed)
- Hyphens, underscores, and other standard characters
- Strings of varying lengths

Almost every barcode scanner on the market reads Code 128 without configuration changes. You do not need to register with any authority or buy any license to use it.

## Practical scenarios for internal barcode labels

**Ecommerce seller with custom SKUs.** You source products and assign your own SKUs. You print barcode labels for incoming stock so your team can scan items during picking and packing.

**Small manufacturer with part numbers.** Each component has an internal part number. You print barcode labels to attach to bins, trays, and product batches in production.

**Retail store with private-label items.** You sell items that did not come with barcodes. You generate internal barcode labels so your POS system can scan them at checkout using your own codes.

**Storage and rental operation.** You assign unique IDs to equipment or inventory. You print barcode labels for check-in and check-out tracking.

## How to generate labels from internal IDs

1. Put your product IDs in a CSV column
2. Include a product name or description in a second column if you want it on the label
3. Go to [Code 128 barcode generator](/code-128-barcode-generator)
4. Paste the CSV and select the ID column as the barcode value
5. Preview the label sheet to confirm all IDs render correctly
6. Export the PDF and print on Avery 5160 label sheets

## Example CSV

```
product_id,name
PART-0041,Mounting Bracket
PART-0042,Cable Anchor
PART-0043,Power Module
```

Select `product_id` as the barcode column. Each part number gets encoded as a Code 128 barcode with the name displayed as label text.

## FAQ

**Can I use sequential numeric IDs as barcodes?**

Yes. A simple numeric sequence like 10001, 10002, 10003 encodes cleanly in Code 128. Many businesses use numeric sequences for simplicity and faster scanning.

**What if my product IDs change over time?**

Generate a new batch of labels when your IDs change. Export an updated CSV with the new IDs, paste it into the tool, and print the new labels. There is no persistent record to maintain.

**Is there a standard format for internal product IDs?**

No formal standard exists. Common conventions use a prefix indicating the category or location (PART-, BIN-, SKU-) followed by a numeric or alphanumeric code. Use whatever format your own system and team find clear and consistent.
